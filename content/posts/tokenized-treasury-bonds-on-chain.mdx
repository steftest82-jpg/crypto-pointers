<h2 id="what-are-tokenized-treasury-bonds">What Are Tokenized Treasury Bonds?</h2>

<p>Tokenized Treasury bonds are digital representations of U.S. government debt instruments issued on a blockchain. Each token is backed one-to-one by an underlying Treasury bill, note, or bond held in custody by a regulated financial institution. This structure gives crypto-native investors access to the stability and yield of government securities without ever leaving the on-chain ecosystem.</p>

<p>The concept emerged from the broader <a href="/blog/real-world-asset-tokenization-investing">real-world asset tokenization</a> movement, which hit $19 billion in total value locked by late 2025. Treasuries quickly became the dominant asset class within that space because of their near-zero default risk and their attractive yield relative to stablecoins sitting idle in wallets.</p>

<p>Unlike stablecoins, which peg to a dollar value but generate no yield by default, tokenized T-bills pass the government yield directly to the token holder. That single distinction has made them one of the fastest-growing categories in DeFi.</p>

<h2 id="how-tokenized-t-bills-work-on-chain">How Tokenized T-Bills Work On-Chain</h2>

<p>The mechanics follow a straightforward custody model. A regulated issuer purchases actual U.S. Treasury securities and holds them at a qualified custodian. The issuer then mints an equivalent number of tokens on a blockchain — typically Ethereum, Solana, or a Layer 2 network — and distributes them to investors who have passed KYC verification.</p>

<p>Yield accrues in one of two ways: either the token price increases daily (a rebasing model), or the yield accumulates separately and is distributed at set intervals. Investors can transfer, lend, or use these tokens as collateral in DeFi protocols, creating composability that traditional bond purchases cannot offer.</p>

<p>Redemption typically takes one to two business days, mirroring the settlement timeline of the underlying government securities. Some platforms offer secondary market liquidity pools for near-instant exits, though these carry small liquidity premiums. For a broader view of how DeFi yield strategies work alongside tokenized assets, see <a href="/blog/best-defi-yield-strategies-2026">best DeFi yield strategies for 2026</a>.</p>

<h2 id="yields-and-returns">Yields and Returns: What to Expect</h2>

<p>Tokenized Treasury yields are not fixed — they float with the federal funds rate. When the Fed held rates between 4.25% and 4.5% through early 2026, most tokenized T-bill products were delivering APYs in the 4.1% to 4.9% range after platform fees. That compares favorably to the 0% to 2% yields available on most idle stablecoins.</p>

<p>Platform fees typically run between 0.10% and 0.50% annually, deducted from the gross Treasury yield before distribution. Ondo Finance charges 0.15% on USDY, while Franklin Templeton's BENJI carries a 0.20% management fee. These figures are modest compared to the yield pickup over doing nothing with a stablecoin.</p>

<p>For context, according to <a href="https://www.sec.gov/cgi-bin/browse-edgar?action=getcompany&type=N-CEN&dateb=&owner=include&count=40" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">SEC filings</a>, traditional Treasury money market funds delivered gross yields of approximately 4.8% in Q1 2026. Tokenized versions are capturing most of that return with only modest additional friction. Investors already familiar with <a href="/blog/passive-income-crypto-7-methods-2026">passive income strategies in crypto</a> will find tokenized T-bills to be among the lowest-risk options on the list.</p>

<h2 id="risks-you-need-to-understand">Risks You Need to Understand</h2>

<p>The risk profile of tokenized Treasuries is genuinely low compared to most DeFi activities, but it is not zero. Smart contract vulnerabilities remain present in any on-chain product — a bug in the token contract or the custody bridge could theoretically expose funds, even if the underlying bonds are safe.</p>

<p>Issuer and custodial risk matters too. The value of the token depends entirely on the issuer correctly managing the underlying bonds and honoring redemptions. Platforms without U.S. regulatory registration carry higher counterparty risk. Always confirm whether a product is registered with the SEC or operates under an exemption.</p>

<p>Liquidity risk deserves attention for larger positions. While secondary market pools exist, they can dry up quickly in risk-off environments. Redemption queues can lengthen when many investors exit simultaneously, and the one-to-two business day settlement window may feel slow during a fast-moving market. Pairing tokenized T-bills with understanding of <a href="/blog/crypto-risk-management-framework">crypto risk management</a> will help you size positions appropriately.</p>

<h2 id="faq">FAQ</h2>

<h3>Are tokenized Treasury bonds available to U.S. investors?</h3>

<p>It depends on the platform. Products like Franklin Templeton's BENJI and BlackRock BUIDL are available to U.S. accredited investors. Ondo Finance's USDY is structured for non-U.S. users, while OUSG is open to qualified U.S. purchasers. Always review the platform's eligibility terms before investing, as KYC and accreditation requirements vary significantly.</p>

<h3>How do tokenized T-bills compare to stablecoin yield farming?</h3>

<p>Tokenized T-bills offer lower yields than aggressive stablecoin farming strategies, which can range from 6% to 15% depending on protocol and market conditions. However, they carry dramatically less smart contract exposure, no algorithmic risk, and the backing of U.S. government credit. For capital preservation with a yield kicker, tokenized Treasuries are the stronger choice. For higher returns with higher risk tolerance, stablecoin strategies on established DeFi platforms can complement them. See <a href="/blog/best-defi-platforms-2026">best DeFi platforms in 2026</a> for a comparison.</p>

<h3>What happens to my tokenized T-bills if the issuing platform shuts down?</h3>

<p>In a properly structured product, the underlying Treasury securities are held in bankruptcy-remote custody separate from the issuer's balance sheet. If the issuer closes, a wind-down process should allow redemption of the underlying bonds. That said, the timeline and process vary by product. Franklin Templeton's BENJI, as a regulated mutual fund, offers the strongest investor protections in this scenario. Always read the fund documents or offering memoranda before committing capital.
